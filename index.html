Our Mission

A new rights body for a new kind of world.

Artificial intelligence is rewriting the rules of creativity, commerce, and daily life at a pace that legislation has not matched. Rights that artists, authors, families, and ordinary people have relied upon for generations are quietly being eroded — not through malice, but through a legal vacuum that no one has yet filled.

FAIR exists to fill it. We are an independent foundation that advocates, researches, and campaigns for enforceable rights across every domain that AI touches: copyright, data protection, children’s safety, and the integrity of human creative work.

We serve creators, rights holders, families, and individuals — anyone whose life intersects with AI. In 2026, that means everyone in the United Kingdom.

AI & the Law

From first recognition
to urgent reform.

A chronology of how the United Kingdom — and the world — has grappled with the legal dimensions of artificial intelligence, from the earliest statutory acknowledgement to today’s legislative impasse.

```
1988
CDPA Enacted — Section 9(3)
The Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 includes Section 9(3), granting copyright in computer-generated works to the person who makes the necessary arrangements — the first UK statutory acknowledgement that machines could produce protectable creative output.
UK Legislation
2011
The Hargreaves Review
Professor Ian Hargreaves’s independent review of IP and growth recommends a text and data mining exception for UK copyright law, identifying it as a barrier to innovation. The government accepts the recommendation.
UK Policy
2013
Section 29A Inserted into the CDPA
The Enterprise and Regulatory Reform Act 2013 adds a text and data mining exception — restricted to non-commercial research only. Commercial AI training remains outside the exception from day one.
UK Legislation
2018
Japan Leads on Commercial TDM
Japan amends its copyright law to permit text and data mining for all purposes including commercial use. The UK does not follow — a divergence that will widen over the following eight years.
International
2019
EU DSM Directive — Articles 3 & 4
The EU creates a mandatory non-commercial TDM exception (Article 3) and a permissive commercial exception with rights-holder opt-out (Article 4). The UK, still a member state, is required to implement it — but leaves the EU before doing so.
European Union
2020
IPO Call for Views: AI and Copyright
The Intellectual Property Office publishes its first formal call for views on AI and copyright, acknowledging deep uncertainty around text and data mining and computer-generated works. No legislative action follows.
UK Government
2021
Singapore Acts; Thaler Reaches Appeal
Singapore enacts a broad commercial TDM exception. The Thaler v Comptroller case reaches the UK Court of Appeal, confirming that patent law requires a human inventor — a structural question only Parliament can resolve.
International  ·  UK Courts
2022
IPO Consultation Response — Two Stated Aims
The IPO articulates two aims in explicit tension: to “unleash the power of AI” and to “preserve the central role of IP in promoting human creativity.” Neither aim is achieved by the legislation as it stands.
UK Government
2023
Thaler — Supreme Court; Getty Filed
The UK Supreme Court unanimously holds that an AI cannot be named as a patent inventor — a matter for Parliament, not the courts. Getty Images simultaneously commences the first major AI training copyright case in England and Wales against Stability AI.
UK Supreme Court
2024
EU AI Act Enters Force; IPO Consults on Section 9(3)
The EU AI Act (Article 53) requires AI model providers to publish training content summaries — a disclosure obligation with no UK equivalent. The IPO simultaneously consults on removing Section 9(3) of the CDPA without a proposed replacement.
EU  ·  UK Government
2025
Getty v Stability AI — Trial Judgment
Mrs Justice Smith holds that AI model weights are not infringing copies of works used for training. Getty’s primary infringement claim is abandoned for lack of evidence that training occurred in the UK — exposing the enforcement gap FAIR was founded to address. Permission to appeal granted.
High Court  ·  England & Wales
2026
OpenAI Pauses Stargate UK  ·  March Report  ·  FAIR Founded
OpenAI pauses its Stargate UK data centre project, citing an unfavourable regulatory environment. The government’s March 2026 Report acknowledges the impasse but proposes no legislation. FAIR is founded with a mandate to build the case for reform that Parliament has so far declined to make.
FAIR Founded
```

Founding Research

The intellectual foundation for everything we do.

FAIR was founded on the arguments advanced in The Alignment Problem, a legal dissertation submitted to the University of Law in April 2026. It demonstrates, through rigorous statutory analysis and systematic legal database research, why the copyright law of England and Wales currently serves neither AI developers nor the rights holders it was designed to protect.

The research draws on comparative analysis of EU, Japanese, and Singaporean frameworks, and on close examination of the landmark Getty Images v Stability AI litigation, to produce four actionable legislative recommendations — the blueprint for FAIR’s flagship campaign.

Completed days after OpenAI paused its UK Stargate data centre project, it is the most current legal analysis of this question available — and it shows precisely what must change.

Chapter 6 — Legislative Recommendations

The Alignment Problem: Artificial Intelligence, Copyright Law, and the Case for Legislative Reform in England and Wales

McCulloch, S. — University of Law, 2026

  • 6.2.1Commercial text and data mining exception with mandatory training-data transparency, modelled on EU DSM Directive Article 4 and AI Act Article 53.
  • 6.2.2Reform of Section 9(3) as a producer right aligned with the post-Infopaq originality standard — targeted correction, not removal.
  • 6.2.3Market-access provision requiring UK copyright compliance for all AI models commercialised domestically, regardless of where training occurred.
  • 6.2.4Comprehensive review of trade secrets, database rights, and UK GDPR as applied to AI training at scale.

```
Case Law Key Decisions
  • Getty Images v Stability AI [2025] EWHC 2863 (Ch)
    First AI training copyright case at trial in England and Wales. Model weights held not to be infringing copies. Primary claim abandoned for lack of evidence of UK training. Appeal pending.
    High Court  ·  2025
  • Thaler v Comptroller-General [2023] UKSC 49
    Supreme Court holds unanimously that an AI system cannot be named as a patent inventor. The broader normative question referred explicitly to Parliament.
    Supreme Court  ·  2023
  • Emotional Perception AI v Comptroller [2026] UKSC 3
    Supreme Court overrules Aerotel and aligns UK patent eligibility with EPO’s G1/19 approach. Inventorship under Thaler remains unresolved.
    Supreme Court  ·  2026
  • THJ Systems Ltd v Sheridan [2023] EWCA Civ 1354
    Leading post-Brexit authority on originality. The only post-2020 case to engage Section 9(3) — resolved on conventional authorial principles without applying it determinatively.
    Court of Appeal  ·  2023
  • Infopaq v Danske Dagblades [2009] ECR I-6569
    CJEU establishes that copyright protection requires the author’s own intellectual creation — creating the post-Infopaq contradiction with Section 9(3) that persists in UK law.
    CJEU  ·  2009
```
