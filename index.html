The Proposal

A new rights body for a new kind of world.

FAIR is an idea, not yet a registered organisation. AI is no longer a specialist tool. It is in classrooms, workplaces, homes and creative studios across the United Kingdom. That access brings enormous potential, but also serious risks that most people have no guidance on how to navigate.

On one side: individuals, parents, students and families who need plain-language guidance on how to use AI safely and what to do if things go wrong. On the other: creators, publishers, businesses and organisations whose intellectual property and data are being used by AI systems without their knowledge or consent.

FAIR proposes to serve both. We want to build the resources, the advocacy and the legal pressure that closes the gap between how AI is being used and how the law protects the people it affects.

Proposed Projects

Six areas. One coalition.

Each of the six proposed projects targets a structural gap where rights are failing. Each is intended to be led by a practising professional working in that space: a lawyer, barrister, academic or specialist who knows the issue from the inside. These are proposals. We are looking for the people to lead them.

Law and Policy

Legislative Reform

UK copyright law fails both AI developers and rights holders. This project proposes four legislative changes: a commercial TDM exception, mandatory training-data transparency, a market-access provision, and reform of Section 9(3) CDPA.

Read the proposals →
Creators

Creator Rights Watch

A proposed monitoring programme tracking how AI companies use the work of authors, journalists, artists and musicians. The aim is to document infringement patterns, publish findings and support creators in understanding their options.

Propose to lead this →
Transparency

Transparent AI

You have the right to know if your work trained an AI model. This project would campaign for mandatory disclosure equivalent to Article 53 of the EU AI Act and provide tools enabling rights holders to verify opt-outs.

Propose to lead this →
Safeguarding

Child Safe AI

Children's voices, images and creative work are being ingested into AI systems with no meaningful protection. This project would advocate for special-category safeguards and work with schools, families and legislators.

Propose to lead this →
Individual Rights

Personal Data Rights

Every person whose data was processed in AI training has rights under UK GDPR. This project would publish plain-language guidance and support individuals navigating data subject access requests against AI companies.

Propose to lead this →
Public Education

Safe and Informed AI

AI is already in classrooms, homes and workplaces. This project would develop free, accessible guidance for parents, students, educators and individuals on responsible AI use, what misuse looks like, and what to do about it, in plain language, not legal jargon.

1988
CDPA Enacted: Section 9(3)
The Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 includes Section 9(3), granting copyright in computer-generated works to the person who makes the necessary arrangements. The first UK statutory acknowledgement that machines could produce protectable creative output.
UK Legislation
2011
The Hargreaves Review
Professor Ian Hargreaves' independent review recommends a text and data mining exception for UK copyright law. The government accepts the recommendation, but the resulting legislation falls well short of what is needed.
UK Policy
2013
Section 29A Inserted into the CDPA
The Enterprise and Regulatory Reform Act 2013 adds a text and data mining exception, restricted to non-commercial research only. Commercial AI training remains outside the exception from day one.
UK Legislation
2018
Japan Leads on Commercial TDM
Japan amends its copyright law to permit text and data mining for all purposes including commercial use. The UK does not follow, and the divergence widens significantly over the following eight years.
International
2019
EU DSM Directive: Articles 3 and 4
The EU creates both a mandatory non-commercial TDM exception and a permissive commercial exception with rights-holder opt-out. The UK, still a member state, is required to implement it, but leaves before doing so.
European Union
2020
IPO Call for Views on AI and Copyright
The Intellectual Property Office publishes its first formal call for views on AI and copyright, acknowledging deep uncertainty around text and data mining and computer-generated works. No legislative action follows.
UK Government
2021
Singapore Acts; Thaler Reaches Appeal
Singapore enacts a broad commercial TDM exception. Thaler v Comptroller reaches the UK Court of Appeal, confirming that patent law requires a human inventor, a structural question only Parliament can resolve.
International and UK Courts
2022
IPO Consultation Response: Two Stated Aims
The IPO articulates two aims in explicit tension: to unleash the power of AI and to preserve the central role of IP in promoting human creativity. Neither aim is achieved by the legislation as it stands.
UK Government
2023
Thaler at Supreme Court; Getty Filed
The UK Supreme Court unanimously holds that an AI cannot be named as a patent inventor, a matter for Parliament, not the courts. Getty Images simultaneously commences the first major AI training copyright case in the UK against Stability AI.
UK Supreme Court
2024
EU AI Act in Force; IPO Consults on Section 9(3)
The EU AI Act requires AI model providers to publish summaries of training content, a disclosure obligation with no UK equivalent. The IPO simultaneously consults on removing Section 9(3) of the CDPA without a proposed replacement.
EU and UK Government
2025
Getty v Stability AI: Trial Judgment
Mrs Justice Smith holds that AI model weights are not infringing copies of works used for training. Getty's primary claim is abandoned for lack of UK training evidence, exposing the enforcement gap FAIR was proposed to address. Permission to appeal granted.
UK High Court
2026
OpenAI Pauses Stargate UK: FAIR Proposed
OpenAI pauses its Stargate UK data centre project, citing an unfavourable regulatory environment. The March 2026 government report acknowledges the impasse but proposes no legislation. FAIR is proposed as a foundation to build the case for reform that Parliament has so far declined to make.
FAIR Proposed
Draft Policy Platform

Four proposed reforms.

These are draft legislative proposals, not final positions. They are put forward to start a conversation and are explicitly open for consultation from rights holders, AI developers, legal professionals and the public.

If you have a view, we want to hear it. The goal is to arrive at positions that are legally rigorous, practically workable and genuinely protective of people's rights.

  • 01Proposed: Commercial TDM exception with mandatory transparency. AI developers should be able to train on content legally, and rights holders should be able to verify their work was used and opt out.Open for consultation. Responses from rights holders and AI developers welcome.
  • 02Proposed: Reform Section 9(3), not remove it. Removing it without replacement creates a legal vacuum around AI-generated works that serves nobody.Open for consultation. Academic and legal responses particularly welcome.
  • 03Proposed: Market-access provision. AI models trained offshore and commercialised in the UK should comply with UK copyright obligations, regardless of where training occurred.Open for consultation. Submissions from trade bodies and law firms welcome.
  • 04Proposed: Comprehensive data governance review. Trade secrets, database rights and UK GDPR as applied to AI training at scale. Copyright reform alone is not enough.Open for consultation. The full picture extends well beyond copyright.
Draft position

Why these four?

The UK government has stated two aims in relation to AI: to unleash its power, and to preserve the central role of intellectual property in promoting human creativity. As of 2026, it is failing at both. These four proposals are a starting point for a debate that Parliament has so far avoided.

They are not claimed as the only answers. They are a basis for conversation.
