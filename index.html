Our Mission

A new rights body for a new kind of world.

Artificial intelligence is rewriting the rules of creativity, commerce, and daily life at a pace that legislation has not come close to matching. Rights that artists, authors, families, and ordinary people have relied upon for generations are quietly being eroded — not through malice, but through a legal vacuum that no one has yet filled.

FAIR exists to fill it. We are an independent foundation that advocates, researches, and campaigns for enforceable rights across every domain that AI touches: copyright, data protection, children's safety, and the integrity of human creative work.

We serve creators, rights holders, families, and individuals — and anyone whose life intersects with AI. In 2026, that means everyone in the United Kingdom.

01 — Flagship Law & Policy

Legislative Reform

UK copyright law fails both AI developers and rights holders. We campaign for four legislative changes grounded in peer-reviewed research: a commercial text and data mining exception, mandatory training-data transparency, a market-access provision closing the extraterritoriality gap, and reform — not removal — of Section 9(3) of the CDPA.

Transparent AI

You have a right to know if your work trained an AI model. We campaign for mandatory disclosure equivalent to Article 53 of the EU AI Act, and provide tools enabling rights holders to query training datasets, verify opt-outs, and pursue remedies through the courts.

Child Safe AI

AI & the Law

From first recognition
to urgent reform.

A chronology of how the United Kingdom — and the world — has grappled with the legal and ethical dimensions of artificial intelligence, from the earliest statutory acknowledgement to today's legislative impasse.

```
1988
CDPA Enacted — Section 9(3)
The Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 includes Section 9(3), granting copyright in computer-generated works to the person who makes the necessary arrangements — the first statutory acknowledgement in UK law that machines could produce protectable creative output.
UK Legislation
2011
The Hargreaves Review
Professor Ian Hargreaves's independent review of IP and growth recommends a text and data mining exception for UK copyright law, identifying it as an obstacle to innovation. The government accepts the recommendation.
UK Policy
2013
Section 29A Inserted into the CDPA
The Enterprise and Regulatory Reform Act 2013 adds a text and data mining exception — but restricted to non-commercial research only. Commercial AI training remains outside the exception from day one.
UK Legislation
2018
Japan Leads on Commercial TDM
Japan amends its copyright law to permit text and data mining for all purposes, including commercial use — subject only to a requirement that copied works are not enjoyed in an ordinary sense. The UK does not follow.
International
2019
EU DSM Directive — Articles 3 & 4
The EU's Directive on Copyright in the Digital Single Market creates a mandatory non-commercial TDM exception (Article 3) and a permissive commercial exception with rights-holder opt-out (Article 4). The UK, still a member state, must implement it — but leaves before doing so.
European Union
2020
IPO Call for Views: AI and Copyright
The Intellectual Property Office publishes its first formal call for views on AI and copyright, acknowledging deep uncertainty around text and data mining, computer-generated works, and the need for reform. No legislative action follows.
UK Government
2021
Singapore Acts; Thaler Reaches Appeal
Singapore enacts a broad commercial TDM exception. Meanwhile, the Thaler v Comptroller case reaches the Court of Appeal, which confirms that UK patent law requires a human inventor — a structural question about AI and rights that only Parliament can resolve.
International · UK Case Law
2022
IPO Consultation Response — Two Stated Aims
The IPO publishes its response to the AI and IP consultation, articulating two aims in explicit tension: to "unleash the power of AI" and to "preserve the central role of IP in promoting human creativity." Neither aim is achieved by the legislation as it stands.
UK Government
2023
Thaler Supreme Court; Getty Filed
The UK Supreme Court unanimously holds that an AI system cannot be named as a patent inventor — a matter for Parliament, not the courts. Separately, Getty Images commences copyright proceedings against Stability AI, the first major AI training infringement case in England and Wales.
UK Supreme Court
2024
EU AI Act Enters Force; IPO Consults on Section 9(3)
The EU AI Act (Article 53) requires providers of general-purpose AI models to publish summaries of training content — a disclosure obligation with no UK equivalent. The IPO simultaneously consults on whether to remove Section 9(3) of the CDPA entirely, without a proposed replacement.
EU · UK Government
2025
Getty v Stability AI — Trial Judgment
Mrs Justice Smith holds that AI model weights are not infringing copies of the works used for training. Getty's primary infringement claim is abandoned for lack of evidence that training occurred in the UK — exposing the structural enforcement gap FAIR was founded to address. Permission to appeal is granted.
High Court · England & Wales
2026
OpenAI Pauses Stargate UK · March Report · FAIR Founded
OpenAI pauses its Stargate UK data centre project, citing an unfavourable regulatory environment. The government's March 2026 Report acknowledges the impasse but proposes no legislation. FAIR is founded on the same month, with a mandate to build the case for reform that Parliament has so far declined to make.
FAIR Founded

Founding Research

The intellectual foundation for everything we do.

FAIR was founded on the arguments advanced in The Alignment Problem, a legal dissertation submitted to the University of Law in April 2026. It demonstrates, through rigorous statutory analysis and systematic legal database research, why the copyright law of England and Wales currently serves neither AI developers nor the rights holders it was designed to protect.

The research draws on comparative analysis of EU, Japanese, and Singaporean frameworks, and on close examination of the landmark Getty Images v Stability AI litigation, to produce four actionable legislative recommendations — the blueprint for FAIR's flagship campaign.

Completed days after OpenAI paused its UK Stargate data centre project, it is the most current legal analysis of this question available — and it shows precisely what must change.

Chapter 6 — Legislative Recommendations

The Alignment Problem: Artificial Intelligence, Copyright Law, and the Case for Legislative Reform in England and Wales

McCulloch, S. — University of Law, 2026

  • 6.2.1Commercial text and data mining exception with mandatory training-data transparency, modelled on EU DSM Directive Article 4 and AI Act Article 53.
  • 6.2.2Reform of Section 9(3) as a producer right aligned with the post-Infopaq originality standard — targeted correction, not removal.
  • 6.2.3Market-access provision requiring UK copyright compliance for all AI models commercialised domestically, regardless of where training occurred.
  • 6.2.4Comprehensive review of trade secrets, database rights, and UK GDPR as applied to AI training at scale.

Key Resources

The legal landscape,
clearly mapped.

The primary legislation, case law, and official reports that define the current framework — and that FAIR is working to change.

```
Legislation Acts & Instruments
  • Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988
    Section 29A (TDM) · Section 9(3) (Computer-generated works) · The foundational UK copyright statute.
    UK · Primary
  • Enterprise and Regulatory Reform Act 2013
    Section 75 — inserted the text and data mining exception into the CDPA, restricted to non-commercial research.
    UK · Primary
  • EU DSM Directive 2019/790
    Articles 3–4 — mandatory non-commercial TDM exception and permissive commercial exception with opt-out. The comparator the UK has declined to follow.
    European Union
  • EU AI Act 2024/1689
    Article 53 — requires providers of general-purpose AI models to publish summaries of training content. No equivalent obligation exists in UK law.
    European Union
  • UK GDPR & Data Protection Act 2018
    The personal data framework governing AI training on data relating to identified or identifiable individuals, including special category data.
    UK · Primary
  • Copyright and Rights in Databases Regulations 1997
    SI 1997/3032 — database right protection for substantial investment in obtaining, verifying, or presenting contents. Relevant to AI training on structured datasets.
    UK · Secondary
Case Law Key Decisions
  • Getty Images v Stability AI [2025] EWHC 2863 (Ch)
    First AI training copyright case to reach trial in England and Wales. Model weights held not to constitute infringing copies. Primary claim abandoned for lack of evidence of UK training. Permission to appeal granted December 2025.
    High Court · 2025
  • Thaler v Comptroller-General [2023] UKSC 49
    UK Supreme Court holds unanimously that an AI system cannot be named as a patent inventor. A human author remains required. The broader normative question referred explicitly to Parliament.
    Supreme Court · 2023
  • Emotional Perception AI v Comptroller [2026] UKSC 3
    Supreme Court overrules the Aerotel test for patent eligibility and aligns UK law with EPO's G1/19 approach. AI inventions involving hardware clear the eligibility threshold more readily — but inventorship under Thaler remains unresolved.
    Supreme Court · 2026
  • THJ Systems Ltd v Sheridan [2023] EWCA Civ 1354
    Leading post-Brexit authority on originality in copyright. The only post-2020 reported case to engage Section 9(3) of the CDPA — though the court resolved the dispute on conventional authorial principles without applying it determinatively.
    Court of Appeal · 2023
  • Infopaq International v Danske Dagblades [2009] ECR I-6569
    CJEU establishes that copyright protection requires the work to be the author's own intellectual creation. Applied in English and Welsh courts post-Brexit, creating the 'post-Infopaq contradiction' with Section 9(3).
    CJEU · 2009
Reports & Research Official & Academic
  • Report on Copyright and Artificial Intelligence (March 2026)
    DSIT & DCMS (CP 2602959). The government's most authoritative review of AI and copyright. Acknowledges the structural gaps — but proposes no legislation to close them.
    UK Government · 2026
  • AI Opportunities Action Plan (January 2025)
    HM Government's statement of its pro-innovation approach to AI regulation — the context against which FAIR's founding research measures the copyright framework's inadequacy.
    UK Government · 2025
  • Copyright and AI: Consultation (December 2024)
    IPO consultation canvassing removal of Section 9(3) as a serious option. No replacement protection proposed. FAIR's position: reform, not removal.
    IPO · 2024
  • ICO: Generative AI — Second Call for Evidence (December 2024)
    ICO finds the lawful basis for processing special category personal data in AI training remains unresolved. Describes the challenge as "particularly challenging" for foundation model developers.
    ICO · 2024
  • Parish — 'Time to Repeal Section 9(3)?' (2025) IPQ
    Archival scholarship drawing on the CDPA's lobbying and drafting history. Reveals the British Computer Society's original objections and clarifies legislative intent. Advance online publication.
    Academic · 2025
  • Hargreaves — Digital Opportunity (2011)
    The independent review of IP and growth that first recommended a TDM exception for UK law. The foundation of Section 29A — and of the reform programme that remains unfinished fifteen years later.
    Independent Review · 2011
