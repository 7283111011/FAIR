Our Mission

A new rights body for a new kind of world.

FAIR is an independent advocacy organisation working to close the legal vacuum that artificial intelligence has created around intellectual property, data rights, children's safety, and creative integrity. We campaign, research, and lobby to ensure UK law protects creators, families, and individuals.

We serve everyone whose life intersects with AI. In 2026, that means everyone in the United Kingdom.

What We Do

Six projects. One purpose.

Each project targets a structural gap where rights are failing and where political change is achievable with the right legal and public pressure.

Law & Policy

Legislative Reform

UK copyright law fails both AI developers and rights holders. We campaign for four legislative changes: a commercial TDM exception, mandatory training-data transparency, a market-access provision, and reform of Section 9(3) CDPA.

Creators

Creator Rights Watch

A monitoring programme tracking how AI companies use the work of authors, journalists, artists, and musicians. We document infringement patterns, publish findings, and support creators in enforcement.

Transparency

Transparent AI

You have the right to know if your work trained an AI model. We campaign for mandatory disclosure equivalent to Article 53 of the EU AI Act and provide tools enabling rights holders to verify opt-outs.

Safeguarding

Child Safe AI

Children's voices, images, and creative work are being ingested into AI systems with no meaningful protection. We advocate for special-category safeguards and work with UK schools and families.

Individual Rights

Personal Data Rights

Every individual whose personal data was processed in AI training has rights. We publish plain-language guidance and support individuals with data subject access requests against AI companies.

Public Education

AI Awareness

Through public campaigns, school partnerships, and accessible publishing, we turn complex legal and technical issues into something every person in the UK can understand and act on.

1988
CDPA Enacted — Section 9(3)
The Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 includes Section 9(3), granting copyright in computer-generated works to the person who makes the necessary arrangements. The first UK statutory acknowledgement that machines could produce protectable creative output.
UK Legislation
2011
The Hargreaves Review
Professor Ian Hargreaves' independent review recommends a text and data mining exception for UK copyright law, identifying it as a barrier to innovation. The government accepts the recommendation — but the resulting legislation falls well short.
UK Policy
2013
Section 29A Inserted into the CDPA
The Enterprise and Regulatory Reform Act 2013 adds a text and data mining exception — but restricted to non-commercial research only. Commercial AI training remains outside the exception from day one.
UK Legislation
2018
Japan Leads on Commercial TDM
Japan amends its copyright law to permit text and data mining for all purposes including commercial use. The UK does not follow, and the divergence widens significantly over the following eight years.
International
2019
EU DSM Directive — Articles 3 and 4
The EU creates both a mandatory non-commercial TDM exception (Article 3) and a permissive commercial exception with rights-holder opt-out (Article 4). The UK, still a member state, is required to implement it — but leaves before doing so.
European Union
2020
IPO Call for Views on AI and Copyright
The Intellectual Property Office publishes its first formal call for views on AI and copyright, acknowledging deep uncertainty around text and data mining and computer-generated works. No legislative action follows.
UK Government
2021
Singapore Acts; Thaler Reaches Appeal
Singapore enacts a broad commercial TDM exception. Thaler v Comptroller reaches the UK Court of Appeal, confirming that patent law requires a human inventor — a structural question only Parliament can resolve.
International and UK Courts
2022
IPO Consultation Response — Two Stated Aims
The IPO articulates two aims in explicit tension: to "unleash the power of AI" and to "preserve the central role of IP in promoting human creativity." Neither aim is achieved by the legislation as it stands.
UK Government
2023
Thaler at Supreme Court; Getty Filed
The UK Supreme Court unanimously holds that an AI cannot be named as a patent inventor — a matter for Parliament, not the courts. Getty Images simultaneously commences the first major AI training copyright case in the UK against Stability AI.
UK Supreme Court
2024
EU AI Act in Force; IPO Consults on Section 9(3)
The EU AI Act requires AI model providers to publish summaries of training content — a disclosure obligation with no UK equivalent. The IPO simultaneously consults on removing Section 9(3) of the CDPA without a proposed replacement.
EU and UK Government
2025
Getty v Stability AI — Trial Judgment
Mrs Justice Smith holds that AI model weights are not infringing copies of works used for training. Getty's primary claim is abandoned for lack of evidence that training occurred in the UK — exposing the enforcement gap FAIR was founded to address. Permission to appeal granted.
UK High Court
2026
OpenAI Pauses Stargate UK — FAIR Founded
OpenAI pauses its Stargate UK data centre project, citing an unfavourable regulatory environment. The March 2026 government report acknowledges the impasse but proposes no legislation. FAIR is founded with a mandate to build the case for reform that Parliament has so far declined to make.
FAIR Founded
Policy Platform

Four reforms. One clear ask of Parliament.

The UK government has stated two aims: to unleash the power of AI technology, and to preserve the central role of intellectual property in promoting human creativity. As of 2026, it is failing at both. FAIR is campaigning for four concrete changes that together would bring UK law into alignment with its own stated ambitions.

  • 01Commercial TDM exception with mandatory transparency. AI developers must be able to train on content legally, and rights holders must be able to verify their work was used and opt out.
  • 02Reform Section 9(3) — not remove it. Removing it without replacement creates a legal vacuum around AI-generated works that serves nobody.
  • 03Market-access provision. AI models trained offshore and commercialised in the UK must comply with UK copyright obligations, regardless of where training occurred.
  • 04Comprehensive data governance review. Trade secrets, database rights, and UK GDPR as applied to AI training at scale. Copyright reform alone is not enough.
