Our Mission

A new rights body for a new kind of world.

FAIR is an independent advocacy organisation working to close the legal vacuum that artificial intelligence has created around intellectual property, data rights, children's safety, and creative integrity. We campaign, research, and lobby to ensure UK law protects creators, families, and individuals, not just the technology industry.

Artificial intelligence is reshaping creativity, commerce, and daily life at a pace that legislation has not come close to matching. Rights that artists, authors, and ordinary people have relied upon for generations are being quietly eroded, not through malice, but through a legal framework that was never designed for what AI is doing today.

We advance legislative reform, submit to parliamentary consultations, build coalitions with rights holders and industry bodies, and lobby Parliament directly. Our non-adversarial position, serving both creators and the AI sector, gives us standing on both sides of the debate and makes our case for reform harder to dismiss.

What We Do

Six projects.
One purpose.

Each project targets a structural gap where rights are failing, and where change is achievable with the right legal and political pressure.

01 Flagship Law & Policy

Legislative Reform

UK copyright law fails both AI developers and rights holders. We campaign for four legislative changes: a commercial TDM exception, mandatory training-data transparency, a market-access provision, and reform of Section 9(3) CDPA.

02 Creators

Creator Rights Watch

A monitoring programme tracking how AI companies use the work of authors, journalists, artists, and musicians. We document infringement patterns, publish findings, and support creators in enforcement.

03 Transparency

Transparent AI

You have the right to know if your work trained an AI model. We campaign for mandatory disclosure equivalent to Article 53 of the EU AI Act, and provide tools enabling rights holders to query training datasets and verify opt-outs.

04 Safeguarding

Child Safe AI

Children's voices, images, and creative work are being ingested into AI systems with no meaningful protection. We advocate for special-category safeguards, work with UK schools and families, and monitor platform compliance with UK GDPR.

05 Individual Rights

Personal Data Rights

Every individual whose personal data was processed in AI training has rights, and almost nobody knows how to exercise them. We publish plain-language guidance and support individuals with data subject access requests.

06 Public Education

AI Awareness

Most people have no idea their data or creative work may have trained a commercial AI model. Through public campaigns, school partnerships, and accessible publishing, we turn legal complexity into something everyone can understand and act on.

1988
CDPA Enacted, Section 9(3)
The Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988 includes Section 9(3), granting copyright in computer-generated works to the person who makes the necessary arrangements. This was the first UK statutory acknowledgement that machines could produce protectable creative output.
UK Legislation
2011
The Hargreaves Review
Professor Ian Hargreaves's independent review of IP and growth recommends a text and data mining exception for UK copyright law, identifying it as a barrier to innovation. The government accepts the recommendation but the resulting legislation will fall short of what is needed.
UK Policy
2013
Section 29A Inserted into the CDPA
The Enterprise and Regulatory Reform Act 2013 adds a text and data mining exception, but restricted to non-commercial research only. Commercial AI training remains outside the exception from day one, a gap that will widen as the AI industry grows.
UK Legislation
2018
Japan Leads on Commercial TDM
Japan amends its copyright law to permit text and data mining for all purposes including commercial use, subject only to a requirement that copied works are not enjoyed in an ordinary sense. The UK does not follow, and the divergence widens over the following eight years.
International
2019
EU DSM Directive, Articles 3 and 4
The EU creates a mandatory non-commercial TDM exception (Article 3) and a permissive commercial exception with rights-holder opt-out (Article 4). The UK, still a member state, is required to implement it but leaves the EU before doing so, forfeiting the most coherent framework available.
European Union
2020
IPO Call for Views on AI and Copyright
The Intellectual Property Office publishes its first formal call for views on AI and copyright, acknowledging deep uncertainty around text and data mining and computer-generated works. No legislative action follows, and the consultation record accumulates without resolution.
UK Government
2021
Singapore Acts; Thaler Reaches Appeal
Singapore enacts a broad commercial TDM exception, becoming another jurisdiction ahead of the UK. Thaler v Comptroller reaches the UK Court of Appeal, confirming that patent law requires a human inventor, a structural question only Parliament can resolve.
International and UK Courts
2022
IPO Consultation Response, Two Stated Aims
The IPO articulates two aims in explicit tension: to "unleash the power of AI" and to "preserve the central role of IP in promoting human creativity." Neither aim is achieved by the legislation as it stands. The response sets the terms of a debate that remains unresolved four years later.
UK Government
2023
Thaler at Supreme Court; Getty Filed
The UK Supreme Court unanimously holds that an AI cannot be named as a patent inventor, a matter for Parliament, not the courts. Getty Images simultaneously commences the first major AI training copyright case in the UK against Stability AI, setting the stage for a landmark judgment.
UK Supreme Court
2024
EU AI Act Enters Force; IPO Consults on Section 9(3)
The EU AI Act (Article 53) requires AI model providers to publish summaries of training content, a disclosure obligation with no UK equivalent. The IPO simultaneously consults on removing Section 9(3) of the CDPA entirely, without a proposed replacement, raising the prospect of making the framework worse.
EU and UK Government
2025
Getty v Stability AI, Trial Judgment
Mrs Justice Smith holds that AI model weights are not infringing copies of works used for training. Getty's primary infringement claim is abandoned for lack of evidence that training occurred in the UK, exposing the enforcement gap FAIR was founded to address. Permission to appeal is granted in December 2025.
UK High Court
2026
OpenAI Pauses Stargate UK, March Report, FAIR Founded
OpenAI pauses its Stargate UK data centre project, citing an unfavourable regulatory environment. The March 2026 government report acknowledges the impasse but proposes no legislation. FAIR is founded with a mandate to build the case for reform that Parliament has so far declined to make. The question is no longer whether the law must change, but when.

Founding Research

The intellectual foundation for everything we do.

FAIR was founded on the arguments advanced in The Alignment Problem, a legal dissertation submitted to the University of Law in April 2026. It demonstrates, through rigorous statutory analysis and systematic legal database research across Westlaw UK, LexisNexis, and BAILII, why UK copyright law currently serves neither AI developers nor the rights holders it was designed to protect.

The research draws on comparative analysis of EU, Japanese, and Singaporean frameworks, and on close examination of the landmark Getty Images v Stability AI litigation. Critically, it goes beyond copyright to show that AI developers simultaneously face exposure under trade secrets law (Vestergaard Frandsen v Bestnet), database rights (Ryanair v PR Aviation), and UK GDPR, none of which provides the clarity the government's stated aims require.

This cumulative analysis produces four actionable legislative recommendations, the blueprint for FAIR's flagship campaign, and demonstrates that copyright reform alone cannot resolve the regulatory uncertainty confronting AI development in the UK.

Chapter 6, Legislative Recommendations

The Alignment Problem: Artificial Intelligence, Copyright Law, and the Case for Legislative Reform in England and Wales

McCulloch, S. University of Law, 2026

  • 6.2.1Commercial text and data mining exception with mandatory training-data transparency, modelled on EU DSM Directive Article 4 and AI Act Article 53.
  • 6.2.2Reform of Section 9(3) as a producer right aligned with the post-Infopaq originality standard, targeted correction, not removal.
  • 6.2.3Market-access provision requiring UK copyright compliance for all AI models commercialised domestically, regardless of where training occurred.
  • 6.2.4Comprehensive review of trade secrets, database rights, and UK GDPR as applied to AI training at scale.

Getty Images v Stability AI [2025] EWHC 2863 (Ch)
First AI training copyright trial in the UK. Model weights held not to be infringing copies. Primary claim abandoned for lack of UK training evidence. Appeal pending.
High Court 2025 View
Thaler v Comptroller-General [2023] UKSC 49
Supreme Court holds unanimously that an AI system cannot be named as a patent inventor. The broader normative question referred explicitly to Parliament.
Supreme Court 2023 View
Emotional Perception AI v Comptroller [2026] UKSC 3
Supreme Court overrules Aerotel and aligns UK patent eligibility with EPO's G1/19 approach. Inventorship under Thaler remains unresolved.
Supreme Court 2026 View
THJ Systems Ltd v Sheridan [2023] EWCA Civ 1354
Leading post-Brexit authority on copyright originality. The only post-2020 case to engage Section 9(3), resolved on conventional authorial principles.
Court of Appeal 2023 View
Vestergaard Frandsen v Bestnet Europe [2013] UKSC 31
Supreme Court confirms liability for misuse of confidential information does not require conscious appreciation of its confidential character. Relevant to trade secrets encoded in AI model weights.
Supreme Court 2013 View
Ryanair v PR Aviation BV (C-30/14) [2015] ECDR 21
CJEU considers what constitutes 'extraction' for database right purposes. Whether a training run processing an entire database constitutes extraction of a substantial part remains judicially untested in the AI context.
CJEU 2015 View
Infopaq v Danske Dagblades [2009] ECR I-6569
CJEU establishes that copyright protection requires the author's own intellectual creation, creating the post-Infopaq contradiction with Section 9(3) that persists in UK law.
